Blue to red in 30 miles
What 1 Pa. county road tells us
about the American electorate

Reporting by Steve Novak | For lehighvalleylive.com
Visuals by Saed Hindash | For lehighvalleylive.com

Northampton County isn’t red. It isn’t blue. It’s purple. And an hour’s drive will show you why.

Pennsylvania State Route 248 spans this crucial swing state county through all political subgroups. One end is in the city of Easton on the Delaware River, an urban area dependably for Democrats. The other end is 30 miles northwest in the reliably Republican rolling hills where the Lehigh River cuts through the Blue Mountain ridge.

The two sides meet in the posh purple townships and industrial boroughs in between. Yard signs for Joe Biden transition to Donald Trump flags as the landscape morphs from urban townhouses to rural farmland, with suburban shopping centers and limestone quarries along the way.

One trip on Route 248 reveals Northampton County’s clear divisions in the 2020 presidential election. Join us on our journey down a single road and see what drives voters in this swing county.

Deciding America’s
direction

Northampton County could decide the 2020 presidential election. In the past century, the candidate chosen by Northampton County voters has assumed the presidency all but three times. It may once again serve as a bellwether for who gets Pennsylvania’s 20 electoral votes.

Take the past three elections, for example. Northampton County twice supported Democrat Barack Obama with 56% of the vote in 2008 and 52% in 2012. In 2016, it became one of only 206 counties in the nation to flip for Republican Donald Trump, giving him 50% of the local vote.

This is a county that political experts say generally encapsulates all of Pennsylvania within just 370 square miles. Compared with the rest of the state and nation, the 300,000 people who live here are generally a little older (the median age is 42), a little less diverse (about 76% white), make a little more money (the median household income is $68,500) and maybe a little more likely to own a home (72% of the local population).

But the picture varies greatly depending on where you view it. Just along our one road, the experiences that influence people’s politics run the gamut from the soul food kitchen owner in Easton, to a teacher living in Nazareth and a corporate retiree in the rural northern tier.

We’ll meet them on our way.

Easton
The true blue

The eastern terminus of Route 248 is the Delaware River in Easton. The route skirts the Downtown area, starting next to the Route 22 bridge where 36,000 drivers enter and leave Pennsylvania each day.

We start here, in this city of 27,000 people, the largest on our journey. It annually celebrates its role in America’s founding — the Declaration of Independence was read aloud in the main hub of Centre Square shortly after its signing — and reliably votes Democrat. The population here is the youngest and most diverse of anywhere on our route. The median age is just 34. More than 15% of Easton is Black and almost a quarter is Hispanic. There is a vibrant Lebanese community that holds an annual public church festival.

Biden signs are most common here, scattered throughout the community a few blocks off Route 248, among Halloween decorations, as we head west.

Signs for Biden and Black Lives Matter are nestled within autumn decorations outside a Ferry Street home in Easton near 15th Street, the border with Wilson Borough.

No Republican presidential candidate has carried even a single precinct in Easton or neighboring Wilson Borough in at least the past 20 years. But what makes the city vote this way? The drive up Route 248 into the West Ward may reveal more.

The diverse
West Ward

Continuing west, we pass Cottingham Stadium. Well, sort of. The Easton Area High School field that dates to the 1920s, a familiar gathering place for generations of local sports fans, is in the midst of a massive renovation after falling into disrepair.

Construction vehicles tear up the field of Cottingham Stadium, off Route 248/Northampton Street in Easton’s West Ward. The stadium is undergoing a major renovation.

The stadium site is in Easton’s West Ward, a large community of row homes arranged in a neat grid of city blocks. Like the field, the West Ward has seen some tough times. It is all too often the scene of drug raids or violent crime. Easton may be young and diverse, but it also has our route’s highest poverty rate at 16.7%.

But there is much good to be found here. City and community leaders have for years emphasized revitalizing the West Ward. And the area’s diversity is seen as a strength, one that works in Biden’s favor.

Jerome Warren runs Yahbie’s Taste of Soul a few blocks from Easton’s border with Wilson Borough. Warren describes the community as racially “mixed-up,” in a good way, a place where people mostly get along. He sees and hears from all sorts of people in his kitchen, including some Trump supporters, but says this community leans Democrat – not necessarily because of Biden’s stances but due to economic instability and racial tension under Trump’s term.

“The whole racial thing that's going on, it's a big problem because [in] this area, this is a lot of mixed people,” says Warren, who is Black. The president, he continued, “touted on the economy. The economy is not doing well. You can't do well when you have racial tension because everybody’s got to get along with everybody.”

The purple
suburbs

After Easton and Wilson Borough, Route 248 takes a northward turn. The landscape changes from urban row homes to malls, shopping centers and offices, with large homes along and just off the main route. Eventually, a cement plant comes into view – the first of many.

Along this stretch, support for Trump starts to appear more. One house declares the area to be “Biden country,” while the gated property just next door displays Trump paraphernalia.

We’re passing through Palmer and Lower Nazareth townships now. Demographically, this is the most well-off area on our drive. Matthew Munsey, chair of the county Democrats, says the party typically gets most of its votes out of the cities and suburbs. That could be key to 2020.

Palmer Township, with 3.5 times the population as Lower Nazareth Township, is a bit more diverse, doesn’t make as much per household (the median income is about $80,000), but is similar in most other demographics. But its votes vary – residents here supported Al Gore in 2000 but were on board with George W. Bush’s re-election in 2004; it went for Obama twice before flipping for Trump in 2016.

Lower Nazareth’s 6,000 people boast the highest median household income ($102,000), the lowest poverty rate (4%) and the highest percentage of college-educated residents (40%) of anywhere along Route 248. The township has gone for Republicans in every presidential election in the past 20 years.

The political battleground reaches its apex as we continue into the borough of Nazareth.

Nazareth
The battleground

Route 248 only skirts the southern edge of Nazareth, a borough with a population about a fifth of Easton’s. To get the true sense of this community, you need to leave the route briefly and head into town.

This is a small borough of 5,700 people, nearly all of whom are white. But when it comes to picking presidents, this community – founded as a religious haven and built around blue-collar industry like the cement plants and now-defunct Nazareth Speedway – is a political battleground. It supported Al Gore in 2000, George W. Bush in 2004 and then backed Obama’s two terms before flipping for Trump in 2016.

Blue and red lines once again have been drawn here. There are a growing number of campaign signs for both Biden and Trump – some right next door to each other, even split down the same duplex.

“That's the beautiful thing about living in our country,” says Beth Bilinski, who lives here and teaches third grade in Easton. She has a Biden flag on her front porch. Her neighbor flies a Trump flag. “It's great that he has that opinion. That's one of our freedoms that our forefathers had fought for so long ago and I'm proud to keep that tradition alive, as I'm sure he is, too. That's what being an American is all about – difference of opinion and being able to be civil about it."

A half-mile away, Juan “Johnny” Rodriguez, a machinist at graphics manufacturer Tapeworks in Bethlehem, has a small Trump banner in the bushes outside his apartment door. As a man of Puerto Rican descent who grew up in the Bronx, he says, people including his own family are surprised by his support for Trump.

“He does a lot of things I don't agree with,” Rodriguez admits. “But I feel like he's better than the other choices.”

Beth Bilinski and her support sign for Biden.

Juan Rodriguez and his support sign for Trump

Asked separately what is most important in this election season to them and their community, both Belinski and Rodriguez independently say the same thing: Fairness for all people. Both also think the election will be close in their modest borough.

“Diversity is beautiful. That's something beautiful about Nazareth,” Rodriguez says.

Bath
Where ‘Trump country’ begins

A cement plant overlooks a cemetery in Bath, a small borough through which Route 248 runs.

Biden signs become scarce as we continue westward. This is where “Trump country,” as some banners call it, really seems to begin.

Following Route 248 through Upper Nazareth Township we come to the tiny borough of Bath, where the local farmers market’s season (Friday evenings, May to September) is advertised on the sign welcoming folks into town. The road passes a cemetery that overlooks a splendid vista that sees all the way to South Mountain on the far side of Bethlehem, eight miles away. The foreground is dominated by yet another cement plant.

Bath is tiny. Fewer than 2,700 people live here. The median income ($45,846) and percentage of college-educated residents (18.1%) are the lowest on our route; the unemployment rate (9.3%) is highest.

Historically, this small electoral prize has not been a gimmie for either party. Bath was blue in the presidential election of 2000, turned red in 2004 for George W. Bush’s second term, flipped again for Obama in 2008, then supported the Republican Mitt Romney in 2012. It stayed red for Trump in 2016.

But if there was much support for Biden here now, it’s not very visible on our drive. “All aboard the Trump train,” says one Broad Street home’s banner, prominently staked in the front yard. Some are more crass (“Trump 2020: No more bullshit,” the flags say) and begin to appear more frequently along our route.

The reliably red
northern tier

As we near the end of our journey, landmarks become few and far between when you enter Northampton County’s northern tier, passing through East Allen, Moore and Lehigh townships. The Pennsville Tavern is one. Becky’s Drive-In is another.

For most of this stretch, you’re just as likely to see a sign declaring a farm’s preservation status as one supporting a candidate, but don’t be fooled. This region is a stronghold for Northampton County Republicans. Gloria “Lee” Snover, who chairs the county GOP, says this region has been “smokin’” when it comes to voter registration in 2020.

These townships have consistently supported Republicans for president in the past 20 years, though some votes have been close. In 2008, John McCain won East Allen by just 20 votes and Lehigh Township by 174. In 2000, George W. Bush won Lehigh Township by 82 votes.

You’ll see Trump signs, and occasionally clusters of banners. Even businesses openly stump for Trump here, including the contractor for which Snover works.

American Property Solutions prominently displays a Trump 2020 trailer and a cardboard cutout of Donald Trump fixed to a mover on its property along Route 248 in Lehigh Township.

Bob Bednarcik has had a Trump 2020 flag on the flagpole for months now, flying just below the American flag. The 70-year-old retiree has spent most of his life in the Lehigh Valley. He worked 40 years training managers in the fast-food industry, and another 10 at a Walgreen’s distribution center. He is confident the area will remain red and thinks Republicans are most likely to follow through on promises that will help people like him, like lowering property taxes.

“I think the people around here are more down to earth and they’re more friendly, like it used to be,” Bednarcik says. “And they realize that the Republicans – Trump – have the same feelings. He’s more concerned about the people.”

Lehigh Twp.
End of the road

We reach the end of our journey at another river. Here, in the northwestern corner of Northampton County, is the Lehigh Gap, where the Appalachian Trail descends the Blue Mountain ridge to cross the Lehigh River.

Route 248 continues a few more miles northwest to its terminus in Carbon County, another county that has been known to swing between parties, but which overwhelmingly supported Trump in 2016, 65% to 31%.

Find an overlook in the gap, and you can look back across Northampton County – potentially one of the single most important counties in the 2020 presidential election. Where history says the rolling hills vote Republican, the cities go for Democrats, and the boroughs and suburbs make up the balance.

What are the voters here looking for? Everyone we spoke to had similar ideas: Fairness. Equality. A voice. They just see different candidates as the way to achieve them.

Route 248 crosses over the Lehigh River, which cuts through the Blue Mountain ridge on the western border of Northampton County. The break in the ridge is known as Lehigh Gap.

“Everybody’s pointing fingers and, just, separating what used to be really good about America,” Bednarcik, the Lehigh Township retiree, says of the current political climate. “Togetherness, working together. If a job needed to get done, everybody got together and did it.”

“I want the person who's going to be president to do the right thing. That's it,” says Rodriguez, the machinist from Nazareth. “Democrat, Republican, independent – just do the right thing. That's what I want."

“It's going to be really close,” says Bilinski, the Easton teacher from Nazareth. “It's going to be exciting to watch democracy in action and … that's what makes America such an amazing place to live.”

"We gotta do better,” says Warren, the soul food kitchen owner in Easton’s West Ward. “And election time is a time for us to go out and vote and do better. … The voting will tell who is fed up and who is not.
